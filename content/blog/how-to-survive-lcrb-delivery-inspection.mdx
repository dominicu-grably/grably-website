An LCRB inspection does not have to be a stressful event. For retailers who have their documentation in order and their processes running correctly, it is a routine administrative review that concludes without incident. For retailers who discover gaps when an inspector arrives, it is something else entirely.

The difference between those two experiences comes down to preparation. This is a practical guide to what that preparation looks like for the delivery side of your operation.

## Before an Inspector Arrives: What to Have Ready

You will not know in advance when a routine inspection is scheduled. The point of unannounced inspections is that retailers operate in compliance continuously, not just when they expect scrutiny. That means the preparation described here is ongoing rather than event-specific.

**Your delivery log must be current and accessible.** Every delivery your store has completed should have a corresponding record in a system you can access and export quickly. If producing your delivery log for last Tuesday would require you to gather information from multiple sources, your log is not ready for inspection.

**Driver records must be current.** For every driver on your team, you need a current Selling It Right certification on file and documentation confirming they are an employee, not a contractor. Keep these records organized and accessible, not filed in a folder you would need to search.

**Your delivery manifests must exist and be organized by date.** If the LCRB asks for delivery manifests for a specific period, you need to be able to produce them quickly. Manifests that were never created cannot be retroactively produced.

**Age verification records must be part of each delivery record.** The inspector may ask to see not just that deliveries occurred, but that age verification was conducted for each one. If your delivery records do not include age verification documentation, that gap will be visible.

## During the Inspection: How to Conduct Yourself

Inspectors are professionals doing a regulatory job. Treating the inspection as an adversarial encounter is neither accurate nor useful. The following approach serves retailers well.

Cooperate fully and promptly. Provide access to records when requested without delay. If you need a moment to retrieve a document, say so clearly and retrieve it promptly.

Answer questions accurately. If you do not know the answer to a question, say so. Guessing or providing information you are not certain of creates risk if the answer turns out to be wrong.

Do not volunteer information beyond what is asked. Answer the question that was asked, completely and accurately, and wait for the next question. This is not about being evasive. It is about keeping the inspection focused on what is actually being reviewed.

If staff are present during the inspection, brief them beforehand on how to respond if they are asked questions directly. Staff who are cooperative and informed reflect well on the operation.

## Common Points Where Retailers Run Into Trouble

Based on publicly available LCRB enforcement decisions and the patterns visible in waiver summary reports, the following are the most common points where delivery operations create compliance problems during inspections.

**Delivery records that exist in informal systems.** A WhatsApp thread, a shared notes document, or a spreadsheet maintained by one employee is fragile documentation. It may be incomplete, inconsistently formatted, and difficult to produce quickly under inspection conditions.

**Age verification that happened but was not recorded.** Drivers who check ID as a matter of habit but do not document the check leave the store without evidence that the check occurred. The inspection record reflects the documentation, not the driver's intentions.

**Failed deliveries with no record.** An order that went out and came back without a documented reason for the failed attempt is an unaccounted order. Inspectors notice these gaps.

**Drivers whose Selling It Right certifications have lapsed.** Certifications expire and renewal is the retailer's responsibility. A driver operating with an expired certification is a violation even if they have been with the store for years.

## After the Inspection: Understanding Your Options

If the inspection concludes without any findings, you will receive confirmation from the LCRB and the matter closes. If violations are identified, you will receive a Notice of Enforcement Action.

At that point, you have two primary options. You can respond to the notice with additional information or context that you believe is relevant to the finding. Or you can sign a waiver accepting the enforcement outcome.

Signing a waiver is often faster and avoids a hearing process, but it is not cost-free. A waiver creates a permanent record on your LCRB file. If a subsequent inspection finds additional violations, your prior waiver will be part of the context the LCRB considers.

Before signing any enforcement document, it is worth taking the time to understand exactly what you are accepting and what alternatives exist. The LCRB's <a href="https://www2.gov.bc.ca/gov/content/employment-business/business/liquor-regulation-licensing/cannabis" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" className="underline hover:text-grably-accent">cannabis retail compliance resources</a> provide information on the enforcement process and your rights within it.

## The Mindset That Makes Inspections Manageable

Retailers who experience inspections as routine rather than stressful share a common characteristic. Their compliance documentation is a byproduct of their daily operations, not a separate administrative burden.

When every delivery automatically generates a timestamped record, when age verification is a required step that drivers complete as part of the delivery flow, when manifests are produced at the end of every run, the documentation exists before anyone asks for it.

That is the operational posture that makes an unannounced LCRB inspection something a retailer can handle confidently rather than something they dread.
