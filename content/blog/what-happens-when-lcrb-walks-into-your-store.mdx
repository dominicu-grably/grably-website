For most licensed BC cannabis retailers, an LCRB inspection is something they have heard about but never experienced firsthand. That changes quickly once a store is operating. The LCRB conducts inspections of every licensed private retailer on a routine basis, and the process is more structured than most owners expect.

Understanding what an inspection actually involves is one of the most practical steps a retailer can take. Preparation is not about gaming the system. It is about knowing what the standard is before someone arrives to measure you against it.

## How Inspections Are Initiated

LCRB inspectors conduct both announced and unannounced visits. Routine compliance inspections are typically unannounced. Targeted enforcement operations, such as the Minors as Agents Program, are always unannounced by design.

Inspectors carry official identification and are authorized under the Cannabis Control and Licensing Act to enter any licensed premises during business hours. You are required to cooperate, provide access to your records, and make staff available to answer questions.

A retailer who refuses entry or obstructs an inspection creates a separate compliance issue on top of whatever the inspector came to assess.

## What Inspectors Review on Arrival

The inspection process follows a consistent structure. Inspectors work from a standardized checklist that covers both physical premises requirements and documentation requirements. For a typical retail store inspection, the review includes the following areas.

**Licence and signage.** Your cannabis retail licence must be posted prominently and visibly. Required signage, including health warnings and age restriction notices, must be displayed correctly and in the required locations.

**Staff certifications.** Every employee involved in the sale or delivery of cannabis must hold a valid Selling It Right certification. Inspectors will ask to see certification records for staff on shift and may request records for all employees. An expired or missing certification is a violation regardless of how long the employee has been with the store.

**Cannabis register.** Your cannabis register must be current, accurate, and available for review. Inspectors compare register entries against your inventory to identify discrepancies. Gaps between recorded and actual inventory are a serious compliance concern.

**Restricted areas and floor plan.** Your store layout must match the approved floor plan on file with the LCRB. Access controls to restricted areas must be functioning and enforced. If you have made any physical changes to the store since your licence was issued, those changes may require prior LCRB approval.

**Delivery records.** For stores operating a delivery service, inspectors will request delivery records as part of the review. This is where many retailers are caught underprepared.

## The Delivery Records Review

The delivery records portion of an inspection is the area where independent BC retailers most commonly have gaps. Inspectors are looking for a complete and organized set of records that demonstrates your delivery operation is being run in accordance with licence conditions.

Specifically, they want to see records that confirm the following for each delivery completed.

The identity of the driver who completed the delivery. The address where the delivery was made. The date and time of the delivery. The items included in the order. Evidence that age verification was conducted at the point of handover. The outcome of the delivery, whether completed or not, and if not, the documented reason.

If those records exist across multiple systems, if they are in a driver's phone, a spreadsheet, and a WhatsApp chat simultaneously, producing them quickly and coherently under inspection conditions is genuinely difficult. Inspectors are not required to wait while you compile records from separate sources.

## The Minors as Agents Program

A significant portion of LCRB inspections are conducted under the Minors as Agents Program. In these inspections, an inspector is accompanied by a person under 19 who attempts to purchase cannabis or receive a cannabis delivery without being asked for identification.

For delivery operations, this means an inspector may send a test order to a delivery address and observe whether the driver checks ID at the door. If the driver hands over the order without requesting identification, the store is in violation regardless of what age verification happened at the point of online sale.

The Minors as Agents Program is the most direct test of whether your age verification process is actually working in practice, not just on paper.

## What Happens After the Inspection

Once the inspection is complete, the inspector prepares a report. If no violations are found, the inspection closes without further action. If violations are identified, the LCRB issues a Notice of Enforcement Action outlining the contraventions and the proposed consequence.

Retailers have the option to respond to the notice or to sign a waiver accepting the enforcement outcome. Signing a waiver is often faster and avoids a formal hearing, but it creates a permanent record on the retailer's LCRB file. That record is publicly searchable through <a href="https://www.canlii.org/en/bc/bclcrb/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" className="underline hover:text-grably-accent">CanLII</a> and does not expire.

For a first-time minor violation, a written warning is a common outcome. For more serious contraventions, or violations that have been identified previously, fines and temporary suspension are more likely. The LCRB publishes its penalty guidelines, and inspectors apply them consistently.

## The Practical Implication for Delivery Operators

The retailers who move through LCRB inspections most smoothly share a common characteristic: their compliance records are organized, current, and accessible before an inspector arrives. They are not scrambling to find records. They are not explaining why documentation exists in three different places. They can produce what is asked for in minutes.

For delivery operations specifically, that level of readiness requires a system that creates compliance records automatically as part of the delivery workflow, not one that requires manual assembly after the fact.
