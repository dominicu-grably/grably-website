Over the past couple of years, the LCRB has conducted hundreds of inspections of private cannabis retail stores across British Columbia — routine compliance checks alongside targeted enforcement operations. For an industry that sometimes treats compliance as a background concern, that volume of inspection activity should reframe the conversation. The LCRB is not waiting for complaints. It is running an active, ongoing inspection programme, and delivery operations are increasingly in scope.

## What the Inspection Data Actually Shows

The LCRB's inspection activity breaks into two broad categories: routine compliance inspections and targeted enforcement operations.

Routine inspections cover the standard compliance checklist. Valid licence posted, Selling It Right certifications current, cannabis register maintained, restricted areas enforced, signage compliant. These are the inspections most retailers have some familiarity with.

The more significant category for delivery operators is the Minors as Agents Program (MAP), active since March 2023, where the LCRB sends an inspector accompanied by a person under 19 to test whether store staff and delivery drivers are verifying age correctly. A substantial share of all LCRB cannabis inspections fall under this programme.

For retailers running delivery, that means the age check at the door is not a theoretical compliance requirement. It is an active, ongoing enforcement priority.

## What Inspectors Are Looking For

Understanding the inspection checklist is the first step toward being ready for one. Based on publicly available LCRB documentation, inspectors reviewing a delivery operation are looking for evidence of the following.

**A complete delivery log.** Every delivery your store completes must have a corresponding record showing the driver's identity, the delivery address, the date and time, the items delivered, and the outcome. This log must be producible on request, not assembled after the fact from text messages and spreadsheet fragments.

**Documented age verification at the door.** Age verification at the point of online sale is necessary but not sufficient. The LCRB requires that ID is checked at the point of handover, by the driver, at the customer's door, and that this verification is documented in your delivery records. A driver who checks ID without recording it leaves your store with no evidence the check occurred.

**Failed delivery documentation.** If a delivery cannot be completed, whether the customer is not home, appears intoxicated, cannot be located, or refuses the order, that outcome must be documented with a reason code and timestamp. The product must be returned to the store. An undocumented failed delivery is a gap in your compliance record.

**Delivery manifests.** A delivery manifest is a consolidated document covering all stops in a single driver run. It is a requirement under the LCRB Cannabis Retail Store Licence Handbook, not an optional best practice.

**Selling It Right certifications for delivery drivers.** Every person involved in the sale or delivery of cannabis must hold a valid Selling It Right certification. This applies to drivers as much as it applies to store staff. An uncertified driver is a compliance violation regardless of how well the rest of your delivery operation is documented.

## The Enforcement Consequences

When inspectors find a store out of compliance, the LCRB issues a Notice of Enforcement Action. Outcomes range from written warnings for minor first-time contraventions to substantial fines and temporary licence suspension for more serious or repeated violations.

What is less widely understood is what happens to that enforcement record. A retailer who signs a waiver accepting an enforcement outcome, often the path of least resistance, creates a permanent entry in their LCRB file. That record is public, searchable, and follows the licence for its lifetime. It does not reset when a store changes ownership or management.

For retailers who have invested significantly in their licence, location, and brand, a permanent enforcement record represents a real and lasting cost that extends well beyond any fine amount.

## The Delivery-Specific Gap Most Retailers Are Carrying

The inspection data points to a consistent pattern: retailers who are broadly compliant on in-store operations are often significantly exposed on delivery. The reasons are structural.

In-store compliance is largely handled by your POS system. It enforces ID checks at point of sale, maintains your cannabis register, and generates the transaction records that form the backbone of your compliance documentation. Your store staff are trained on it. The workflows are embedded in daily operations.

Delivery compliance has no equivalent system for most BC retailers. Orders leave the store, and from that point forward the compliance record depends entirely on manual processes: driver texts, WhatsApp messages, spreadsheets, and institutional memory. None of those produce documentation that survives an LCRB inspection.

The inspection programme does not distinguish between retailers who are non-compliant because they are cutting corners and retailers who are non-compliant because their tools were never built for the task. The outcome is the same.

## What Preparedness Actually Looks Like

A retailer who is genuinely prepared for an LCRB delivery inspection can do the following without advance notice.

Produce a complete delivery log for any date in recent memory within five minutes. Show documented age verification records for every delivery on that log. Demonstrate a process for handling and documenting failed deliveries. Provide delivery manifests for every run in the period under review. Confirm that every driver on the log holds a valid Selling It Right certification.

If any of those five items would require significant preparation time, there is a compliance gap worth addressing before an inspector arrives.

## The Practical Step Forward

The retailers who fare best in LCRB inspections are not necessarily the ones with the most elaborate compliance programmes. They are the ones who have made compliance documentation automatic, built into the daily workflow of every dispatch and every delivery, rather than something assembled after the fact when an inspector arrives.

That shift, from manual record-keeping to automatic documentation, is the operational change that most meaningfully reduces delivery compliance risk for independent BC cannabis retailers.
